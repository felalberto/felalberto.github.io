Teacher-facing pages

Teacher Resource 1

Teacher Overview

Use this overview to understand the project before introducing it to students.

CodeBronx
Code the Moment

What students will make

Students choose one small moment from a story and turn it into a short Scratch animation using characters, setting, dialogue, movement, sequence, and debugging.

Best classroom fit

Use during narrative writing, character study, retelling, dialogue, sequence of events, point of view, or creative response to text.

CategoryRecommendation
Grade BandGrades 3 to 5. Adaptable for grades 2 to 6.
Time Needed45 minutes for a quick activity, 2 class periods for a mini-project, or 3 class periods for a full build and share cycle.
ToolScratch. Students may work individually or in pairs.
Final ArtifactA short animated scene that communicates one important story moment.
ELA FocusCharacter, setting, dialogue, action, sequence, theme, point of view, retelling, or narrative development.
CS/CT FocusDecomposition, sequencing, events, algorithms, debugging, and optional loops or costume changes.
Teacher reminder: The goal is not for students to code an entire story. The goal is for students to select one focused scene and make the story thinking visible through code.

Adaptation Guide

Connect the project to different ELA goals and Scratch skill levels.

CodeBronx
Adaptations
ELA FocusStudent TaskTeacher Question
Character TraitsAnimate a moment that shows what a character is like.What does the character say or do that reveals their trait?
SequenceAnimate the beginning, middle, or end of one important scene.What has to happen in order for this moment to make sense?

Student Worksheet 1

Student Project Sheet

Turn one small moment from a story into a Scratch animation.

Name: ___________
Date: ___________

Your challenge

Choose one important or interesting moment from a story. Then use Scratch to bring that moment to life with characters, setting, dialogue, movement, and sequence.

  1. Choose a story.
  2. Pick one small moment.
  3. Identify characters, setting, actions, and dialogue.
  4. Break the moment into smaller steps.
  5. Plan how each step will look in Scratch.
  6. Build, test, debug, and share.
  • I chose one focused story moment.
  • I included characters and setting.
  • I used dialogue or narration.
  • I showed action or movement.
  • My scene happens in a clear order.
  • I tested and debugged my project.

Student Worksheet 5

Scratch Build + Debug Checklist

Use this while building and testing your Scratch project.

Name: ___________
Date: ___________
  • At least 2 sprites
  • A relevant backdrop
  • Dialogue using say blocks
  • Movement or action
  • An event to start the scene
  • Correct sequence from beginning to end
  • Animation or costume change
  • Testing and debugging
StepWhat I Did
1. Read the code out loud.
2. Test one sprite at a time.
3. Pull apart blocks that are not working.
4. Add blocks back one at a time.
5. Ask: Did the actions happen in the correct order?
